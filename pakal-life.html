Chapter 1

A King is Born

26th March, 603 CE
K'inich Janaab' Pakal is born
In the city of Palenque, deep in the jungle rainforest, a royal baby was born who would one day become the greatest ruler the Maya world had ever seen.

Imagine being born into a world of towering pyramid temples, sacred jaguars, and a sky full of gods. That was the world of K'inich Janaab' Pakal — the child who would later be known to us as Pakal Votan.

Pakal was born on 26th March 603 CE in the great Maya city of Palenque, hidden deep in the rainforests of what is now southern Mexico. His name, K'inich Janaab' Pakal, means "Sun Shield" — a name fit for a king.

"The Maya believed their kings were descended from the gods — a link between heaven and earth."

As a royal child, Pakal grew up learning everything about Maya life — how to read glyphs, how to perform religious ceremonies, and how to lead a people. He was being prepared for a destiny that would last 68 years on the throne.

Chapter 2

Crowned at Twelve

615 CE — Pakal is aged just 12
The youngest king in Palenque's history
When the crown was placed on Pakal's head, nobody could have guessed he would reign for 68 years — longer than almost any ruler in the ancient world.

In 615 CE, when Pakal was only 12 years old, he was crowned King of Palenque. Can you imagine being crowned a king or queen at your age? Pakal was barely older than you when he took the throne!

The city of Palenque had been through very difficult times. Rival cities had attacked and defeated it. The people were weak and the city was struggling. Many people must have looked at the young boy on the throne and wondered if he was strong enough to save them.

"He was crowned at 12. He would still be ruling at 80."

But Pakal proved everyone wrong. Over the decades that followed, he rebuilt Palenque from the ground up — creating new temples, palaces and monuments that made it one of the most beautiful cities in the ancient world.

At 12, Pakal couldn't rule alone. His mother Lady Sak K'uk' helped govern the city alongside him. The Maya had a rule that only men could be king — but Lady Sak K'uk' found a way around this by claiming she was a direct descendant of the gods, which gave her the authority to place the crown on her son's head.

This was Maya politics at its cleverest — using religion, stories and belief as tools of power.

Chapter 3

The Great Builder

Pakal Votan was not just a warrior or a ruler — he was one of the greatest builders in all of Maya history. During his 68-year reign, he transformed Palenque into a city of extraordinary beauty.

He ordered the construction of massive temples, palaces and plazas, all decorated with intricate stone carvings and painted in brilliant colours. These were not just buildings — they were messages to the gods, and to anyone who visited Palenque, that this was one of the greatest cities on Earth.

The Temple of Inscriptions — Pakal's most famous building, containing his tomb
The Palace of Palenque — a complex of buildings Pakal expanded and beautified

One of Pakal's greatest achievements was the Palace of Palenque — a huge complex of rooms, corridors and courtyards, topped with a four-storey tower that was probably used to watch the stars. Pakal covered the walls with carved stone portraits showing himself as a god-king.

But his most remarkable building project was one that would only be completed after his death. He began constructing a great pyramid temple — the Temple of Inscriptions — that would eventually become his tomb. Imagine ordering your own tomb to be built, knowing it would take decades and would only be finished long after you were gone!

"The walls of Palenque told the story of Pakal's divine right to rule — written in stone forever."

Chapter 4

The Tomb and the Legend

Pakal Votan died in 683 CE at the remarkable age of around 80 years old — extraordinary for the ancient world, where most people lived much shorter lives. He had reigned for 68 years.

He was buried deep inside the Temple of Inscriptions — the great pyramid he had begun building decades earlier. His body was placed in a magnificent stone coffin called a sarcophagus, lowered down a secret staircase hidden inside the pyramid, and sealed away for over 1,000 years.

The sarcophagus was discovered in 1952 by Mexican archaeologist Alberto Ruz Lhuillier, who spent four years carefully excavating the staircase. When he finally opened the tomb, it was one of the most extraordinary archaeological discoveries of the 20th century.

683 CE · Temple of Inscriptions
The most famous Maya carving ever found
The carved lid of Pakal's sarcophagus is one of the most detailed and beautiful objects the Maya ever created — packed with meaning about life, death and rebirth.

The lid of Pakal's stone coffin is covered in an intricate carving that tells the story of his journey after death. At the centre is Pakal himself, shown falling down into Xibalba — the Maya underworld. But this is not just a sad story — the Maya believed that death was not the end.

Rising above Pakal in the carving is the World Tree — a great tree that connected the underworld, the earth and the heavens. A serpent with two heads stretches across the scene, and at the very top of the tree sits a sacred quetzal bird, symbol of the sky.

"Just as the maize seed falls into the ground and rises again as a plant, so Pakal would be reborn."

Around the edge of the lid are the lords of the underworld — fearsome beings who Pakal would face on his journey. The entire carving was a message of hope: that Pakal, like the maize god, would rise again.

Chapter 5

Pakal's Legacy

Pakal Votan died in 683 CE at approximately 80 years old — but his story didn't end there. The city of Palenque continued to flourish after his death. His son, K'inich Kan B'alam II, completed the Temple of Inscriptions and added more magnificent buildings in his father's honour.

Over 1,300 years later, Pakal's name is still known around the world. The Temple of Inscriptions is now one of the most visited archaeological sites in Mexico, and people travel thousands of miles to stand before the same pyramid that Pakal built as his eternal resting place.

The carved sarcophagus lid is now considered one of the most important objects ever created by the ancient Maya. It has been studied, photographed, and reproduced millions of times. The original still lies in the tomb beneath the Temple of Inscriptions — exactly where it was placed in 683 CE.

Pakal's legacy is everywhere in Maya history. He showed that a city could rise from near-defeat to become the most beautiful in the world. He showed that writing, art and architecture could be used to tell stories that last thousands of years. And he showed that even a 12-year-old can change the world.

"The Maya didn't think time moved in a straight line — they believed the past, present and future were all connected. Pakal understood this. His buildings were messages to the future — messages we are still reading today."
